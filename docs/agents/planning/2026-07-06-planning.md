## Today's Question

How do agents decompose, revise, and execute long-horizon tasks? What are the core paradigms for planning with LLMs?

## Main Paper

### Metadata

- Title: Understanding the planning of LLM agents: A survey
- Authors: Xu Huang, Weiwen Liu, Xiaolong Chen, Xingmei Wang, Hao Wang, Defu Lian, Yasheng Wang, Ruiming Tang, Enhong Chen
- Year: 2024
- Venue: arXiv
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02716

### Why this paper?

Day 1 of a topic should start with a survey. This is the first systematic survey of LLM-based agent planning, providing a taxonomy that maps the entire space. It is the ideal entry point for building a mental map of the topic.

### Core Problem

LLMs demonstrate impressive capabilities in reasoning and language understanding, but planning — the ability to decompose a long-horizon goal into a sequence of actions, revise when things go wrong, and execute reliably — remains a fundamental bottleneck. The survey asks: how can LLMs be leveraged as planning modules, and what are the remaining gaps?

### Main Idea

The paper introduces a **planning process framework** with three stages:

1. **Task Decomposition** — Breaking down a complex goal into manageable subgoals or steps. Approaches range from single-pass decomposition (chain-of-thought style) to iterative refinement. Key methods include:
   - **Decomposition with feedback**: LLM generates a plan, then revises based on environmental feedback or self-critique.
   - **Decomposition with external tools**: Using classical planners (e.g., PDDL solvers) or task-specific algorithms to assist decomposition.
   - **Decomposition through learning**: Fine-tuning LLMs on planning trajectories to internalize decomposition patterns.

2. **Plan Selection** — Choosing which action or subplan to execute next. Methods span:
   - **Single-path selection**: Standard autoregressive generation of the plan.
   - **Multi-path selection**: Tree/graph search over alternative plans (ToT, GoT, MCTS-inspired approaches).
   - **External verifier**: Using a separate model or learned reward function to rank candidate plans.
   - **Feedback-driven revision**: Selecting the next action based on execution results, not just plan coherence.

3. **Plan Execution & Re-planning** — Carrying out actions and recovering from failures:
   - **Reactive execution**: Simple step-by-step execution with no lookahead.
   - **Dynamic re-planning**: Monitoring execution and adjusting the remaining plan when deviations occur.
   - **Hierarchical execution**: High-level planner decomposes tasks, low-level policies execute primitive actions (classic hierarchical planning).

The survey also categorizes planning approaches by the **planning horizon** (short vs. long), **plan granularity** (abstract vs. concrete), and **integration depth** (LLM as planner vs. LLM as part of a planning system).

### Key Technical Details

- The survey covers 40+ papers organized by the three-stage framework.
- **Decomposition patterns**: Chain-of-Thought (linear), Tree-of-Thoughts (divergent-explore), Plan-and-Solve (explicit decomposition then solve), Least-to-Most (sequential decomposition guided by solved subtasks).
- **External planner integration**: LLM+P (Liu 2023) translates natural language goals into PDDL, uses classical planners for sound planning, then maps back to natural language. This hybrid approach overcomes LLM hallucination in planning but requires a formal domain definition.
- **Reflection-based approaches**: Inner Monologue (Huang 2022) uses environmental feedback to revise plans; Reflexion (Shinn 2023) — already covered in architectures — applies verbal reinforcement learning to improve planning through past experience.
- **Search-based planning**: Tree-of-Thoughts and Graph-of-Thoughts maintain multiple partial plans and use heuristic-guided search; MCTS-guided planning uses Monte Carlo Tree Search to balance exploration vs. exploitation in the planning space.
- **Key datasets and benchmarks**: ALFWorld (text-based household tasks), VirtualHome (activity planning), TaskBench (LLM agent task completion), PlanBench (dedicated planning benchmark).

### Research takeaway

LLM planning is not one problem but a spectrum. At one end, LLMs can generate reasonable plans zero-shot for simple tasks; at the other end, complex long-horizon tasks still require hybrid systems combining LLMs with classical planners, learned verifiers, and hierarchical architectures. The fundamental tension: LLMs are flexible but unreliable for multi-step planning, while classical planners are reliable but inflexible.

### Modern perspective

Since this survey (Feb 2024), the field has advanced significantly. Key developments include:

- **RL-based planning fine-tuning**: GRPO and similar methods (used in DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1) train LLMs to plan through verifiable outcome rewards, internalizing planning capabilities at the parameter level rather than relying solely on in-context planning.
- **Code-as-action planning**: Planning is increasingly done through code (generating and executing Python scripts) rather than natural language action sequences, enabling better composition and error recovery.
- **Agent-native benchmarks**: New benchmarks like SWE-bench, AgentBench, and WebArena test planning in realistic environments with execution feedback.
- **Implicit planning**: Models like DeepSeek-R1 demonstrate that extended chain-of-thought reasoning at test time can substitute for explicit planning in many tasks.

## Related Papers

### Paper 1: Plan-and-Act — Improving Planning of Agents for Long-Horizon Tasks

- **Authors**: Lutfi Eren Erdogan, Nicholas Lee, Sehoon Kim, Suhong Moon, Hiroki Furuta, Gopala Anumanchipalli, Kurt Keutzer, Amir Gholami
- **Year**: 2025
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09572

**Contribution**: Proposes a **separate planning phase** performed before execution, distinct from the execution phase. The planner generates a high-level plan (sequence of abstract steps), then the executor carries them out with low-level tool calls. This decoupling prevents the model from losing sight of the overall goal during multi-step execution.

**Relation to main paper**: Directly addresses the survey's observation that single-pass generation (without separate planning) fails on long horizons. Plan-and-Act concretely implements the "task decomposition → plan selection → execution" pipeline described in the survey's framework.

**Why it matters**: A clean, practical architecture that demonstrates the benefit of explicit planning separation. Unlike ReAct (which interleaves thought/act/observe at each step), Plan-and-Act commits to a plan first, then executes. This trades flexibility for reliability — exactly the tradeoff the survey identifies.

**Deep read later?**: Yes. The decoupled planning architecture is a common pattern across many modern agent frameworks. Understanding its limits (when does the plan need revision?) is important.

### Paper 2: Beyond Entangled Planning — Task-Decoupled Planning for Long-Horizon Agents

- **Authors**: Yunfan Li, Bingbing Xu, Xueyun Tian, Xiucheng Xu, Huawei Shen
- **Year**: 2026
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.07577

**Contribution**: Identifies a key failure mode in existing planning approaches — **entangled planning**, where task decomposition, step sequencing, and tool selection are all handled by a single planning pass, creating cascading errors. Proposes **task-decoupled planning** that separates what to do (task decomposition) from how to do it (tool selection and execution), using different LLM calls or modules for each.

**Relation to main paper**: Extends the survey's taxonomy by identifying a specific failure pattern within the "task decomposition" stage. Where the survey catalogs approaches, this paper diagnoses a fundamental weakness and proposes a targeted fix.

**Why it matters**: "Entangled planning" is a useful conceptual lens for understanding why many agent planning attempts fail — the same model that decomposes the task also selects tools, and errors in decomposition propagate to tool selection. Decoupling is an elegant solution with practical implications.

**Deep read later?**: Yes. The concept of decoupling applies broadly — not just to planning but potentially to reasoning, memory, and action selection.

## Current Understanding

Planning is **the bottleneck** for LLM agents on long-horizon tasks. The field has converged on a three-stage framework: decompose, select, execute/re-plan. Each stage has its own challenges:

1. **Task decomposition** — LLMs can generate plausible subgoals but often miss steps, make them too abstract, or fail to account for dependencies between subtasks. Hybrid approaches (LLM + classical planner) are promising but require formal domain specifications.
2. **Plan selection** — Multi-path selection (search over plans) beats single-path selection but is expensive. Verification-based approaches (learned reward models, environment simulators) can filter bad plans before execution.
3. **Re-planning** — The most understudied stage. Most systems assume the plan will work or do simple retry on failure. Dynamic re-planning — adjusting the remaining plan based on partial execution results — remains an open challenge.

The key insight from comparing the survey with newer work: **decoupling** (separating planning from execution, decomposition from tool selection) is an emerging design principle that addresses the reliability gap without abandoning LLM flexibility.

## Key Concepts

- Task decomposition: breaking long-horizon goals into subgoals
- Plan selection: choosing among alternative actions/subplans
- Re-planning: adjusting plans based on execution feedback
- Planning horizon: the temporal length of planning (short vs. long)
- Plan granularity: abstract vs. concrete action specification
- Entangled planning: handling decomposition, selection, and execution in one pass
- Decoupled planning: separating what-to-do from how-to-do-it
- Hybrid planner: LLM + classical planner (e.g., LLM+P)
- Search-based planning: ToT, GoT, MCTS for plan space exploration
- Reflection-guided planning: using past experience to improve future plans
- Verification-based planning: learned/external verifiers to filter plans

## Open Questions

1. Can LLMs ever be reliable enough for zero-shot planning on complex tasks, or will hybrid (LLM + classical planner) systems always be necessary?
2. What is the optimal granularity of decomposition — too fine creates too many steps, too coarse misses critical subproblems?
3. How should re-planning be triggered? At fixed intervals? On failure only? When model uncertainty drops?
4. Can search-based planning (ToT, MCTS) scale to real-world tasks with hundreds of steps?
5. Is planning best handled by the same model as execution, or should planners and executors be specialized (and how specialized)?
6. How should planning interact with memory — can planning benefit from reusing past plans for similar tasks?
7. What is the right evaluation for planning? Current benchmarks (PlanBench, ALFWorld) are narrow — real-world planning involves uncertainty, partial observability, and dynamic environments.
8. Can RL fine-tuning (GRPO, outcome reward models) give LLMs genuine planning capabilities, or just better imitation of planning patterns?

## Possible Thesis Ideas

1. **Adaptive planning depth** — dynamically decide whether to generate a full plan upfront or plan opportunistically (interleaved execution) based on task complexity and environmental uncertainty. This maps to the survey's observation that different tasks need different planning strategies.

2. **Plan-as-code** — encode plans as executable programs rather than natural language step lists, leveraging code structure (loops, conditionals, exception handling) for robust plan execution and re-planning. Extends the CodeAct paradigm into the planning domain.

3. **Experience-driven planning** — build a plan library from past execution traces, using retrieval to adapt past plans to new tasks instead of generating plans from scratch. Combines planning with the memory topic.

4. **Verifier-guided plan search** — train a learned verifier (separate from the planner) that predicts plan success probability before execution, enabling efficient rejection of bad plans without costly environment interaction.
