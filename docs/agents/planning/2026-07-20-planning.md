## Main Paper

### Metadata

- Title: What We Talk About When We Talk About LLM Planning: Evidence for Two Distinct Planning Abilities
- Authors: Sukai Huang, Chenyuan Zhang, Fucai Ke, Zhixi Cai, Naim Rastgoo, Gholamreza Haffari, Hamid Rezatofighi
- Year: 2026
- Venue: arXiv
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11197

### Why this paper?

This paper is the ideal capstone for the Planning topic. It directly addresses the meta-question that emerged across our previous readings: *why do LLMs perform unevenly across planning tasks*? Rather than attributing gaps to task difficulty, it uses multidimensional Item Response Theory (IRT) to uncover latent competency structure. The finding — two distinct planning abilities with different scaling properties — reframes how we should evaluate and improve LLM planning.

### Core Problem

When LLMs perform well on some planning tasks but poorly on others, the standard explanation is "task difficulty." This paper argues that task-level variation reflects **distinct latent planning competencies** rather than differences along a single difficulty axis. The problem: without understanding what competencies are actually being measured, planning evaluations conflate multiple unrelated abilities, making it impossible to determine whether an intervention (scaling, RL, CoT) genuinely improves planning or just improves one narrow competency.

### Main Idea

The authors study planning on **ACPBench-Hard**, a benchmark of planning tasks, across multiple LLM families (from 8B to 671B parameters) under varying test-time reasoning budgets. They apply a **multidimensional Item Response Theory (IRT)** model to uncover the latent competency structure.

The analysis reveals **two principal dimensions**:

1. **Operational Reasoning** — The ability to evaluate local action applicability and immediate state transitions. This is about "what action is valid right now given the current state." It improves with model scaling and longer reasoning traces.

2. **Structural Enumeration** — The ability to reason about goal reachability and landmark structure. This is about "what sequence of actions can reach the goal from the current state." It remains **comparatively insensitive** to model scale and reasoning budget.

### Technical Details

- **Benchmark:** ACPBench-Hard — a set of planning problems designed to isolate different aspects of planning ability
- **Models evaluated:** Multiple LLM families across scales (8B to 671B)
- **Intervention:** Varying test-time reasoning budgets (number of tokens allowed for CoT reasoning before producing the answer)
- **Analytical method:** Multidimensional IRT, which models each test item as loading on multiple latent abilities, and estimates each model's proficiency on each latent dimension
- **Key result:** Operational reasoning and structural enumeration load on distinct factors; operational reasoning shows strong correlation with scale and reasoning budget, structural enumeration shows weak or no correlation

### Research takeaway

The paper shifts the planning debate from "do LLMs improve on planning tasks overall" to **"which planning competencies improve, under what conditions, and why."** This is a crucial reframing. Our earlier exploration showed that different planning paradigms address different aspects of the planning problem — hybrid planning (LLM+P) handles structural constraints via PDDL, search-based planning (ToT/MCTS) explores alternative sequences, latent planning compresses structure into representations. The IRT framework explains why: these paradigms target different underlying competencies.

### Modern perspective

This paper pairs naturally with "Chasing Progress, Not Perfection" (Huang et al., 2024) — same first author, same research thread. The 2024 paper found that strategies like CoT, fine-tuning, and RL improve plan executability but not plan validity. The 2026 paper explains why: CoT and RL primarily improve **operational reasoning** (local action choices), while **structural enumeration** (global plan validity / goal reachability) remains stubbornly resistant. This suggests that future planning research should focus on targeted interventions for structural enumeration, not just more of the same scaling or reasoning strategies.

The emergent response planning paper (Dong et al., 2025) adds another layer: LLMs encode future outputs in their hidden representations, suggesting a form of implicit structural awareness even before explicit reasoning begins. But the IRT findings suggest this implicit awareness may not translate into structural enumeration competence — a fascinating tension.

## Current Understanding

Planning for LLM agents is not a single ability. We now have evidence for **two distinct competencies**: operational reasoning (local action choice, improves with scale + reasoning) and structural enumeration (global goal reachability, resists scaling/reasoning). This reframes the entire planning landscape:

- **Pure LLM planning** — Strong at operational reasoning, weak at structural enumeration. Works for simple tasks but not for tasks requiring long-horizon planning.
- **Hybrid LLM+classical (LLM+P, NL2Plan, TAPE)** — Uses classical planners for structural enumeration, LLMs for NL-to-formal translation. Directly addresses the structural enumeration gap.
- **Hierarchical/HTN planning** — Decomposes structural enumeration into subtasks. The hierarchy makes goal reachability more tractable but has the domain acquisition bottleneck.
- **Hierarchical+compression (HIPIF)** — Information folding helps with context management but doesn't directly improve structural enumeration.
- **Implicit/latent planning** — LLMs encode future outputs in hidden representations, but this implicit knowledge may not transfer to explicit structural enumeration.

The key insight: **benchmarking planning as a single score conflates two fundamentally different abilities.** Future evaluations should report operational reasoning and structural enumeration separately.

## Key Concepts

- Structural enumeration — goal reachability and landmark structure
- Multidimensional Item Response Theory (IRT) — models test items as loading on multiple latent abilities
- ACPBench-Hard — planning benchmark isolating different competency dimensions
- Test-time reasoning budget — tokens allocated for CoT reasoning before answer

## Open Questions

- Can structural enumeration be improved through targeted training, or is it fundamentally constrained by the next-token-prediction objective?
- Does RL with process reward models (PRM) selectively improve one competency over the other?
- Is structural enumeration the same as "plan validity" from the Chasing Progress paper, or are they different constructs?
- Can we design architectures where operational reasoning is handled by the LLM and structural enumeration by a separate verifier/planner module?
- Does the implicit planning found by Dong et al. (2025) actually encode structural information that could be surfaced?

## Possible Thesis Ideas

1. **Competency-aware planning architecture** — A planning system with two modules: an LLM for operational reasoning (action selection) and a lightweight verifier for structural enumeration (goal reachability checking), trained separately with different rewards.
2. **Structural enumeration via process reward models** — Train a PRM specifically to evaluate structural properties of partial plans (landmark coverage, subgoal ordering), then use it to guide LLM planning via search.
3. **Competency-targeted RL for planning** — Design an RL training curriculum that explicitly separates operational and structural competencies, using different reward components for each.
4. **Probing-to-explicit transfer for structural planning** — Extract the implicit structural knowledge from LLM hidden representations (Dong et al.) and fine-tune the model to produce explicit structural plans, testing whether the representational knowledge can be surfaced.
